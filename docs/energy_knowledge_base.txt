SECTION 1 — CANADIAN ELECTRICITY PRICING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Ontario Time of Use Pricing (IESO)
Off-Peak Hours: 7.4 cents per kilowatt hour
Applies overnight from 7pm to 7am weekdays
and all day weekends and holidays.
This is the cheapest time to charge batteries and EVs.

Mid-Peak Hours: 11.3 cents per kilowatt hour
Applies from 7am to 11am and 5pm to 7pm on weekdays.
Medium cost — avoid large loads if possible.

On-Peak Hours: 17.0 cents per kilowatt hour
Applies from 11am to 5pm on weekdays.
Most expensive time — use stored battery power instead.

Alberta Pool Price (AESO)
Alberta uses a real-time market price that changes every hour.
Average price is approximately 8.5 cents per kilowatt hour.
Prices spike during cold winter mornings and hot summer afternoons.
Battery storage is very valuable in Alberta due to price volatility.

BC Hydro Time of Use
Off-Peak: 9.4 cents per kilowatt hour
On-Peak: 14.2 cents per kilowatt hour
Peak hours are 4pm to 9pm on weekdays.


SECTION 2 — BATTERY ENERGY STORAGE (BESS) GUIDELINES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Safe Operating Range
Always keep battery between 20% and 90% state of charge.
Never discharge below 20% — damages battery cells permanently.
Never charge above 90% — reduces long term capacity.
This range preserves battery life for 10 to 15 years.

Charging Strategy
Charge battery during off-peak hours when electricity is cheapest.
In Ontario charge between 7pm and 7am.
Use solar energy to charge during daytime when available.
Never charge battery during on-peak hours unless emergency.

Discharging Strategy
Discharge battery during on-peak hours to avoid expensive grid electricity.
In Ontario discharge between 11am and 5pm.
Also discharge during grid frequency events when IESO requests.
Keep minimum 20% reserve for emergency backup power.

Battery Health Monitoring
Monitor state of charge every 15 minutes.
Alert when SOC drops below 25% — approaching minimum safe level.
Alert when SOC exceeds 88% — approaching maximum safe level.
Track number of charge cycles — lithium batteries last 3000 to 6000 cycles.
One full cycle per day = 10 to 16 years of battery life.


SECTION 3 — SOLAR PV GUIDELINES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Expected Performance
Solar panels produce maximum output at solar noon — typically 12pm to 1pm local time.
Output follows a bell curve — low in morning high at noon low in evening.
Zero output at night and during heavy snow coverage.
Cloud cover reduces output by 10% to 80% depending on cloud thickness.

Performance Factors
Panel efficiency: typically 18% to 22% for modern panels.
Temperature coefficient: output decreases 0.3% to 0.5% per degree Celsius above 25°C.
Inverter efficiency: typically 96% to 98%.
Dust and soiling: reduces output by 2% to 5% — clean panels quarterly.

Troubleshooting Low Output
If solar output is lower than expected check these causes in order:
1. Cloud cover — check weather forecast for irradiance data
2. Snow or ice on panels — inspect and clear if safe to do so
3. Panel soiling or bird droppings — schedule cleaning
4. Inverter fault — check inverter display for error codes
5. Shading from new obstructions — trees or new buildings nearby
6. Panel degradation — normal 0.5% per year reduction in output

Canadian Solar Resources
Ontario average solar irradiance: 4.2 peak sun hours per day
Alberta average solar irradiance: 4.8 peak sun hours per day
BC average solar irradiance: 3.8 peak sun hours per day
Saskatchewan average solar irradiance: 5.0 peak sun hours per day


SECTION 4 — WIND TURBINE GUIDELINES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Wind Power Basics
Wind turbines start generating at cut-in wind speed of 3 to 4 metres per second.
Maximum output at rated wind speed of 12 to 14 metres per second.
Turbine shuts down automatically above 25 metres per second for safety.
Output follows cube law — doubling wind speed increases power by 8 times.

Small Wind Turbine Performance
Capacity factor for small turbines: 20% to 35% of rated capacity on average.
Wind is more variable than solar — wider forecast uncertainty.
Wind often blows stronger at night — complementary to solar generation.
Annual energy production = rated capacity x capacity factor x 8760 hours.

Maintenance Schedule
Monthly: visual inspection of blades and tower
Quarterly: check blade condition and bolt torques
Annual: professional inspection of gearbox and generator
Every 5 years: major overhaul of all mechanical components


SECTION 5 — IESO ONTARIO MARKET PARTICIPATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

How to Register for IESO Markets
Step 1: Contact IESO at ieso.ca and request market participant registration
Step 2: Complete technical qualification testing of your system
Step 3: Install IESO-approved interval metering at point of common coupling
Step 4: Sign market participant agreement with IESO
Step 5: Complete IESO training courses for market participation
Step 6: Submit day-ahead offers through IESO Energy Trading System
Process takes approximately 3 to 6 months from application to first trade.

IESO Demand Response Program
IESO runs seasonal demand response auctions twice per year.
Participants commit to reduce load by a specified amount when called.
Payment has two components: availability payment and performance payment.
Availability payment: paid monthly just for being registered and ready.
Performance payment: paid when actually called to reduce load.
Minimum size: 100 kilowatts for direct participation.
Smaller systems can participate through an aggregator like GreenGrid AI.

Hourly Ontario Energy Price (HOEP)
Real-time wholesale electricity price updated every hour.
Available at ieso.ca under market data section.
Used to calculate optimal battery dispatch timing.
When HOEP exceeds 10 cents per kilowatt hour consider selling battery power.


SECTION 6 — AESO ALBERTA MARKET PARTICIPATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

How to Register for AESO Markets
Step 1: Apply for market participant registration at aeso.ca
Step 2: Complete AESO registration forms and technical requirements
Step 3: Install approved metering and communication equipment
Step 4: Pass AESO system access service tests
Step 5: Register each generating unit separately with AESO
Process takes approximately 2 to 4 months.

Alberta Energy Pool Price
Real-time price updated every hour — highly volatile.
Ranges from near zero to 999 Canadian dollars per megawatt hour.
Highest prices occur during cold snaps in January and February.
Battery storage extremely valuable for capturing price spikes.
Price data available at aeso.ca under market and system reporting.

Alberta Micro-generation Program
Systems under 5 megawatts can register under micro-generation regulation.
Excess electricity credited against your bill at retail rate.
No complex market registration required for small systems.
Administered through your local distribution company.


SECTION 7 — NERC CIP CYBERSECURITY REQUIREMENTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What is NERC CIP
NERC CIP stands for North American Electric Reliability Corporation
Critical Infrastructure Protection standards.
These are mandatory cybersecurity requirements for grid-connected systems.
Violations can result in fines of 1000 to 1000000 Canadian dollars per day.

Basic Requirements for Small DER Operators
CIP-002: Identify which of your systems connect to the grid.
Document your solar inverters battery management system
and any communication devices connected to grid networks.

CIP-003: Create a cybersecurity policy document.
Designate one person responsible for cybersecurity.
Review and update policy annually.

CIP-005: Protect your network boundary.
Use a firewall between your DER systems and the internet.
Only allow necessary network connections.
Document all electronic access points.

CIP-007: Keep software updated.
Apply security patches within 35 days of release.
Disable all unnecessary network ports and services.
Use strong passwords — minimum 15 characters.
Enable logging of all login attempts.

CIP-011: Protect sensitive information.
Do not share system configuration details publicly.
Store system passwords securely — use a password manager.
Shred or securely delete documents containing system details.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Ontario Net Metering
Program: Net Metering through local distribution company
Maximum system size: 250 kilowatts
How it works: excess electricity credited to next bill
Credit rate: retail electricity rate
Annual settlement: unused credits expire each April
How to apply: contact your local distribution company

British Columbia Net Metering
Program: BC Hydro Net Metering
Maximum system size: 100 kilowatts
How it works: excess electricity credited at BC Hydro retail rate
Annual settlement: April reconciliation each year
How to apply: bchydro.com net metering application

Alberta Micro-generation
Program: AUC Rule 024 Micro-generation
Maximum system size: 5 megawatts
How it works: excess electricity offset against consumption
Credit rate: current regulated rate option rate
How to apply: contact your local distribution company

Saskatchewan Net Metering
Program: SaskPower Net Metering
Maximum system size: 100 kilowatts
Monthly credit settlement
How to apply: saskpower.com net metering

Manitoba Net Metering
Program: Manitoba Hydro Net Metering
Maximum system size: 100 kilowatts
Annual credit settlement
How to apply: hydro.mb.ca net metering

Claim through corporate income tax return


SECTION 10 — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why is my solar output lower than expected today?
A: The most common causes are cloud cover reducing irradiance,
snow or dirt on panels, inverter operating in reduced power mode
due to high temperature, or shading from nearby objects.
Check the weather data on the Forecasting page for current
irradiance levels and compare to your actual output.

Q: When is the best time to charge my battery tonight?
A: In Ontario the cheapest electricity is available from
7pm to 7am. Set your battery to charge between 11pm and 5am
for maximum savings when overnight demand is lowest and
prices are at their minimum.

Q: How do I join the IESO demand response program?
A: Contact IESO at ieso.ca and request information about
the Demand Response Auction. You need at least 100 kilowatts
of controllable load or generation. GreenGrid AI can aggregate
your system with other small operators to meet this minimum.
The registration process takes 3 to 6 months.

Q: What is my carbon offset worth in Canadian dollars?
A: Each tonne of CO2 avoided is worth approximately 50 to 80
Canadian dollars in the voluntary carbon market. Your solar
system avoids approximately 0.21 kilograms of CO2 per kilowatt
hour generated. A 500 kilowatt system generating 600 megawatt
hours per year avoids 126 tonnes of CO2 worth approximately
6300 to 10000 Canadian dollars annually.

Q: Should I upgrade from 300 kWh to 500 kWh battery?
A: This depends on your daily energy pattern. If your battery
regularly reaches 90% charge by noon and discharges to 20%
by 9pm you are constrained by capacity and an upgrade makes
sense. If your battery rarely reaches above 70% you have
excess capacity and upgrading would not improve economics.
Check the Live Dashboard battery SOC chart for guidance.

Q: Am I eligible for the Canada Greener Homes Grant?
A: The grant is available to Canadian homeowners for primary
residences only — not commercial properties. If your DER
installation is at your home you may qualify for up to
5000 Canadian dollars. Apply at canada.ca before starting
any new work as retroactive applications are not accepted.

Q: How much is my frequency regulation service worth?
A: For a 150 kilowatt battery registered for frequency
regulation with IESO you can expect approximately 120 to
300 Canadian dollars per month in availability payments
plus additional performance payments when called upon.
Registration takes 3 to 6 months through IESO.
